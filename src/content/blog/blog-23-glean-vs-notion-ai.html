Glean vs Notion AI: Which Is Right for Your Enterprise Team?

Glean and Notion AI both show up in enterprise AI evaluations, but they're solving different problems. Glean is an AI search layer built to index and surface content across your entire connected tool stack. Notion AI is an AI layer built into a workspace tool to help you create, summarize, and query content that already lives there. Picking the wrong one isn't a disaster, but it leads to a gap between what teams expect and what the product actually delivers. This post breaks down exactly what each does well, where each falls short, and how to decide.

What Glean Is Actually Built For

Glean's core job is org-wide search. Connect it to your Confluence, Google Drive, Slack, GitHub, Salesforce, Jira, and whatever else your team uses — and give everyone a single natural-language interface to find things across all of it. Ask "what's our policy on enterprise refunds?" and Glean retrieves the relevant Confluence page, Slack thread, and email thread, synthesizes an answer, and cites the sources.

This is a genuine problem worth solving. Knowledge scattered across twelve tools is effectively invisible. Glean makes it findable without requiring anyone to remember which tool a piece of information lives in.

What Glean doesn't do: it doesn't execute actions, run workflows, or scope knowledge to specific teams. Its permission model inherits from source tools — if you can see it in Confluence, you can find it in Glean. That's not a flaw for broad enterprise search, but it becomes a constraint for teams that need tighter control over who reasons over what.

What Notion AI Is Actually Built For

Notion AI is an assistant inside Notion. It helps you draft content, summarize long pages, answer questions about your Notion workspace, generate action items from meeting notes, and auto-fill database properties. If you're already spending significant time in Notion, it compresses a lot of common tasks.

The constraint is the boundary: Notion AI knows what's in Notion. It doesn't know what's in Slack, Jira, your CRM, or your email. For teams whose workflow and knowledge are concentrated in Notion, that boundary doesn't matter much. For teams where Notion is one of five tools and not even the primary one, it matters a lot.

The Core Difference

Glean Indexes Everything. Notion AI Deepens One Thing.

Glean is horizontal: broad coverage across your whole tool stack, shallow depth per tool. Notion AI is vertical: deep integration within one workspace, zero coverage outside it. Neither is wrong — they're just different bets on where your knowledge bottleneck actually is.

When Glean Wins

Glean is the right choice when the primary problem is fragmentation — knowledge scattered across many tools that people can't find efficiently. Specifically:

  • Your team uses 5+ tools and knowledge is distributed across all of them
  • You have a large content library (thousands of pages, years of Slack history) that needs to be searchable
  • You want a single search interface for the whole organization, not just one team
  • Your primary use case is retrieval, not creation or automation

When Notion AI Wins

Notion AI wins when your team's knowledge is concentrated in Notion and the bottleneck is productivity within that workspace, not cross-tool retrieval:

  • Your runbooks, playbooks, meeting notes, and project docs all live in Notion
  • You want AI drafting, summarization, and Q&A inside your existing workspace without a new tool
  • Budget is a constraint — Notion AI adds marginal cost to an existing Notion subscription
  • Your team doesn't need cross-tool reasoning or workflow automation

When Neither Is Enough

Both Glean and Notion AI share the same fundamental gap: they retrieve and surface information, but they don't act on it. Neither can trigger an automated workflow, route a generated output for human approval, or execute a multi-step agent process based on the knowledge they surface.

Teams that have outgrown the "find the answer" paradigm and need their AI layer to actually do something — draft a client email from account history, categorize an incoming ticket, generate a weekly report and send it — need a platform built around workflow execution, not just knowledge retrieval.

The Verdict

If your team's knowledge is primarily in Notion and you need AI to make working inside Notion faster, choose Notion AI. If your knowledge is spread across many tools and people spend real time searching for things that exist somewhere, choose Glean. If you need the AI to reason over scoped team knowledge and then take action on what it finds, look at platforms built around workflow execution rather than retrieval.

The worst outcome is buying the broad-search tool when your problem is workspace productivity, or buying the workspace AI when your problem is cross-tool fragmentation. Know your bottleneck first, then pick the tool that solves it.
